The Dual Basis

  • 25 June 2025

The tangent space and the metric lets us do basic calculus at a point on our surface: vector displacements with the metric telling us distances and angles.

The next step is to "do calculus" with scalar functions on our surface. At a point on our surface, we can approximate a scalar function with a Linear Functional These are what we are going to need:

f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y) f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)

The Dot Product

There is a deep relationship between the Dual Space and the Tangent Space (hence the "dual"). This is through a notion of the dot product, which you can define on the tangent space using the Metric Tensor.

With the Tangent space and the Metric Tensor, we can determine the geometry of our surface in its coordinate system by using the Metric Tensor to define a dot product, which encapsulates the notion of distance of vectors and the angle between vectors.

The Dual Basis

The elements of the Dual Space are linear functionals. But for any vector vvv in the Tangent space, we can use it to define a linear function, interpreting it as a linear function where for any tangent space vector xxx

v(x)=v⋅x v(x) = v \cdot x v(x)=v⋅x

For an orthonormal basis of a vector space, the dot product has a nice property that you can use it to compute the component of any vector vvv along a basis vector eee just by taking v⋅ev \cdot ev⋅e

The basis of our tangent space might not be nice in this sense. The {ei}\{e_i\}{ei​} might not be unit vectors, but more significantly they might not be orthogonal at all.

But we can find such a basis - the dual basis {ei}\{e^i\}{ei} where this property holds, and it will be unique.

We want for any vector vvv in our tangent space to have components xix^ixi in {ei}\{e_i\}{ei​} so that

v=∑i(v⋅ei)eiv = \sum_i (v \cdot e^i) e_i v=i∑​(v⋅ei)ei​

Two interpretations of the Dual Basis

Vectors in the tangent space are naturally thought of as column vectors, taking for v=∑ixieiv=\sum_i x^i e_iv=∑i​xiei​ the column vector

x= [x1⋮xn]x=\ \begin{bmatrix} x^1 \\ \vdots \\ x^n \end{bmatrix} x= ​x1⋮xn​​

A linear transformation on the tangent space has a straightforward formulation as matrix multiplication.

v′=Avv' = \mathbf{A}v v′=Av

Now the dual basis {ei}\{e^i\}{ei} of the Dual Space, which is a space of linear functionals, which means we interpret eie^iei as the functional with

ei(ej)=δjie^i(e_j) = \delta^i_j ei(ej​)=δji​

But also the {ei}\{e^i\}{ei} are actual vectors in our tangent space, computed by

with the defining relationship

ei⋅ej=δjie^i \cdot e_j = \delta^i_j ei⋅ej​=δji​

So we can also think of them as column vector in the tangent space itself.
