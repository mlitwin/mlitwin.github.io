The Tangent Space

  • 24 May 2025

Let's set the scene of the things I think I understand, at least to the first approximation. We have a curvilinear coordinate system on a space under some metric. For example, the surface of a sphere. We will start simple, and then mess with it to look at a slightly more complicated variant.

Coordinates

Define a Spherical Coordinate System. For a given point take:

  • r=r =r= radius
  • θ=\theta =θ= polar angle (angle from the north pole)
  • ϕ=\phi =ϕ= azimuth (angle from the prime meridian)

In our example we will let r=1r=1r=1.

Coordinate System

  • The lines of longitude (θ\thetaθ is constant) are great circles through the poles
  • The lines of latitude (ϕ\phiϕ is constant) are perpendicular to the longitudes

Cartesian Coordinates

For the conversion to cartesian coordinates we have:

  • x=rsin⁡θcos⁡ϕx=r \sin\theta \cos\phi x=rsinθcosϕ
  • y=rsin⁡θsin⁡ϕy=r \sin\theta \sin\phi y=rsinθsinϕ
  • z=rcos⁡θz=r \cos \thetaz=rcosθ

Tangent Space

At each pont you have basis vectors of the tangent space: {e1,e2}\{e^1,e^2\}{e1,e2}.

For our sphere, the basis vectors are perpendicular, so this is a cartesian coordinate.
