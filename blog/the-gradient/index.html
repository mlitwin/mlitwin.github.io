The Gradient

  • 27 July 2025

Now that we have a good dual basis for linear functionals on our tangent space, we can use it to express an important functional - the gradient of a function defined on our surface.

The Gradient

Consider a function F({ui})F(\{u_i\})F({ui​}) on our surface. We can look at the rate of change AiA_iAi​ of FFF along the direction of our tangent vectors eie_iei​, and form the linear functional fff where

∇F=∑iAiei=∑i∂F({ui})∂uiei\nabla F = \sum_i A_ie^i = \sum_i \frac{\partial F(\{u_i\})}{\partial u_i} e^i ∇F=i∑​Ai​ei=i∑​∂ui​∂F({ui​})​ei

The steepest direction

Two Interpretations of the Gradient

Now we have two ways to think about the gradient of a function FFF.

In the dual space, ∇F\nabla F∇F is a linear functional that describes the infinitesimal change of FFF according to the partial derivatives of FFF along the tangent basis vectors eie_iei​.

In the tangent space, ∇F\nabla F∇F is a vector in the direction of the steepest change of FFF, whose length is equal to the rate of change of FFF in that direction.
