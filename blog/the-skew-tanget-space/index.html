The Skew Tangent Space

  • 18 June 2025

Coordinates

Define a Spherical Coordinate System. For a given point take:

  • r=r =r= radius
  • θ=\theta =θ= polar angle (angle from the north pole)
  • ϕ=\phi =ϕ= azimuth angle from the prime meridian, which we will take as the great circle through the north pole and the geomagnetic pole at a distance γ\gammaγ from the north pole.

In our example we will let r=1r=1r=1.

Coordinate System

  • The lines of longitude (θ\thetaθ is constant) are great circles through the poles
  • The lines of latitude (ϕ\phiϕ is constant) are at different angles to the longitudes.

Tangent Space

At each pont you have basis vectors of the tangent space: {e1,e2}\{e^1,e^2\}{e1,e2}.

For our sphere, the basis vectors are not in general perpendicular, so this is a non-cartesian coordinate system.

'

e1e^1e1 is a tangent along a great circle through the magnetic pole, so take that as our unit length a in the cartesian case.

e2e^2e2 is the same as our cartesian case, so we can take it as a unit vector in the x / horizontal direction of magnitude sin⁡θ\sin\thetasinθ.

We need the angle between e1e^1e1 and e2e^2e2. This is an exercise in spherical trigonometry:

Given

  • P=P =P= North Pole
  • M=M =M= Magnetic Pole.
  • γ=\gamma = γ= angle from PPP to MMM along our prime meridian

Let

  • A=A=A= point of contact / origin of our tangent space
  • B=B=B= intersection of latitude line from AAA to the prime meridian
  • θ=\theta=θ= polar angle from PPP to AAA
  • ϕ=\phi=ϕ= azimuth around MMM from BBB to AAA
  • α=∠MAB\alpha = \angle MABα=∠MAB which is the angle we seek.

Let's figure out some of the angles here:

  • Both arc PAPAPA and arc PMBPMBPMB are arcs of great circles through the pole PPP, so they intersect the longitude arc ABABAB at right angles.
  • arc AMAMAM and PMBPMBPMB are arcs of great circles, so ∠PMA+∠AMB=π\angle PMA + \angle AMB = \pi∠PMA+∠AMB=π

And now we can apply the spherical law of sines and some basic trig

sin⁡(π/2−α)γ=sin⁡π−ϕθ\frac{\sin(\pi/2-\alpha)}{\gamma} = \frac{\sin\pi-\phi}{\theta} γsin(π/2−α)​=θsinπ−ϕ​

cos⁡αγ=sin⁡ϕθ\frac{\cos\alpha}{\gamma} = \frac{\sin\phi}{\theta} γcosα​=θsinϕ​

cos⁡α=γθsin⁡ϕ\cos\alpha= \frac{\gamma}{\theta}\sin\phi cosα=θγ​sinϕ

Which is the angle we seek.
